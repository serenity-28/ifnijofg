Nobela ni Jose Rizal:

El Filibusterismo

About the Novel

El Filibusteriso, translated into Subversive or The Subversion, also known as "The Reign of Greed", is Jose Rizal's last novel. It is the sequel to his infamous Noli me Tangere.

The story centers around the protagonist of the previous novel, Crisostomo Ibarra, who—13 years older—has now come back for his vengeance as the sly jeweler, Simoun. The novel tackles themes of revenge, love, and tragedy in the last days of the Spanish regime in the Philippines.

Jose Rizal expands on his message from Noli me Tangere, and goes into the topics of educational reform, revolution, religion, corruption, abuse and the debate between the path of violence and non-violence when it comes to fighting for your country. Our protagonist takes on a path of violence, seeing it necessary for the start of a revolution, a juxtaposition to his previous feeling of peaceful reform. We see the return of old characters and the change (or lack) in them. It is a different and darker atmosphere than before.

The novel, along with its pair, has had a large influence on the Philippines' view on identity, faith, and society as a whole. The Spanish had banned the novel due to its political themes and talk of the issues in the Spanish rule, showcasing the abuse of Filipinos and corruption within the system. Jose Rizal himself was soon exiled to Dapitan then executed because of his writing. The books were smuggled throughout the nation, inciting a fire within the people that gave start to the Philippine Revolution.

Jose Rizal is considered a National Hero by many. His influence is still large till this day, with a lot of subjects in school tackling his life and his works.

Characters
Simoun
Previously Crisosostomo Ibarra, now an infamous and rich jeweler. He is the closest advisor to the Capitan-General, who he became friends with in Cuba. Nobody really likes him but they do like his stock of jewels.

Basilio
A young Filipino man who is studying medicine. He was acquainted with Ibarra when he was a child, involved in the events of the man's downfall. His studies are supported by the rich Capitan Tiago.

Isagani
A batchmate and close friend of Basilio. He is a poet and leader of the Spanish Language Academy (a proposed idea from Filipino students for the creation of an academy to teach Spanish) He is the fiance of Paulita Gómez.

Don Custodio
A Manila politician who as a young man moved to the Philippines from Spain. He is a widely successful man, tightly knit with many organizations, the church, and the Capitan-General himself

Ben Zayb
A Spanish hack journalist who sees himself as the only thinking head in the Philippines. His writing holds a dramatic flair to it that usually leads him to butt heads with the censorship. He is rather arrogant despite being a dishonest journalist.

Padre Irene
A powerful Spanish priest and a close confidant to Capitan Tiago. He enables his opium addiction, to the dismay of Basilio. Rather promiscuous for a priest.

Doña Victorina
A wealthy mestiza socialite, who is the wife of Don Tiburcio and the aunt of Paulita Gómez. She is obsessed with Europeanizing herself and distancing herself as much as she can from her Filipino roots. Her marriage is very unhappy and this leads to her husband running away to the countryside.

Paulita Gómez
A beautiful young woman and like her aunt she is a socialite. She is engaged with Isagani but does not hold his view on Filipino nationalism, preferring the Europe adjacent lifestyle.

Padre Camorra
A young priest in charge of the area around San Diego, like Padre Irene, he is licentious.

Padre Salví
A powerful Spanish priest but on the elderly side. He is an important individual in the downfall of Ibarra, although he does hold some suppressed guilt over what happened 13 years ago.

Padre Sibyla
A powerful and very conservative Spanish priest. He is part of the Dominican order of the Catholic Church.

Capitan Tiago
A rich haciendero with a severe opium addiction. He took Basilio in after incidents in the previous novel and bought all the land that Ibarra used to own.

The Capitan-General
The colonial commander of the Philippines. He was previously stationed in Cuba, where he met his now closest advisor, Simoun. Quite disinterested when it comes to doing his duties as leader.

The Chief of Staff
Unlike many Spanish leaders in the story, he is more welcoming to the idea of reform. He can be seen supporting Isagani and his classmates' idea for the Spanish Language Academy.

Padre Florentino
A one of a kind Filipino priest and uncle to Isagani. He is a kindhearted man and a believer of non-violence.

Cabesang Tales
A farmer and father of Julí and Tanó. His land was seized by the church since he could not pay the rent. He becomes hugely obsessed with taking the land back by any means necessary, hurting his family in the process.

Julí
A young Filipino woman whose father is Cabesang Tales, she is also engaged to Basilio. Due to her father's obsession and her want to raise funds, she goes into servitude under Sister Penchang.

Tandang Selo
Father to Cabesang Tales, and grandfather to his son's two children. When Julí becomes a servant to pay for her father's ransom, he loses his ability to speak.

Father Fernández
A Spanish priest, and one of the few who sympathizes with the Filipinos. He is known as a lively teacher among students and advocates on behalf of the Spanish Language Academy.

Don Tiburcio
A Spaniard, and husband of Doña Victorina. He runs away from his marriage after hitting his wife during an argument.

Juanito Peláez
A mestizo student and son of Don Timoteo. He is a self serving opportunist.

Father Millón
A Dominican friar who teaches physics. He is a cruel teacher, and enjoys embarrassing his students with questions they cannot answer. Even with his superiority complex, he still does not believe what he is teaching.

Plácido Penitente
A Filipino student who finds himself depressed and isolated in university. Father Millón's abuse makes him drop out. He wishes he can travel to Hong Kong.

Makaraig
A wealthy Filipino student and a leader alongside Isagani in their Academy movement. He is a generous person, giving out loans to his fellow students when they are in need.

Quiroga
A successful Chinese merchant with high influence. Yet, his Chinese background causes him to be looked down upon, and this makes him strive to start a Chinese consulate in the city. He owes Simoun a lot of money.

Don Timoteo
The prominent businessman and father of Juanito Peláez. His favorite pastime is to demolish houses and sell the now homeless people building materials.

Mister Leeds
An American that owns what he claims to be a talking head from Egypt.

Señor Pasta
Quiroga's lawyer and a Filipino. Isagani goes to him for help for their Academy movement, he has long given up dreams of freedom for the sake of his career.

Sister Penchang
A deeply religious widow in San Diego. She agrees to loan Julí money, in exchange, she works under her.

María Clara
She was the former fiancée of Ibarra and an integral character in the previous novel. She was sent to the convent after Ibarra's 'demise'.
